FPS vs. Hz: Understanding the Difference

One of the most common misconceptions in the gaming community is treating FPS (Frames Per Second) and Hz (Hertz) as the same metric. While they work together to produce the image you see, they originate from different sources.

1. What is FPS (Frames Per Second)?

FPS is a measure of performance generated by your CPU (Processor) and GPU (Graphics Card). It represents how many unique images your system can render and output every second.

  • Low FPS (< 30): The video will look choppy, like a slideshow. This is considered unplayable for modern gaming.
  • Standard FPS (30-60): The standard for console gaming (PS4, Xbox One) and movies. It is acceptable for casual gaming but may feel sluggish in competitive shooters.
  • High FPS (60-144+): The gold standard for PC gaming. Animations are fluid, and inputs feel snappy.

2. What is Hz (Refresh Rate)?

Hz is a hardware limit of your Monitor or Phone Screen. It represents how many times per second the screen can physically update the pixels.

  • 60Hz: Standard office monitors and older phones. The screen refreshes 60 times a second.
  • 90Hz / 120Hz: Common in modern flagship smartphones (iPhone Pro, Samsung S-Series, OnePlus).
  • 144Hz / 240Hz / 360Hz: Dedicated Esports monitors designed for CS:GO, Valorant, and Overwatch.

What is Screen Ghosting & How to Test It?

Have you ever moved your mouse quickly or turned your camera in a game, only to see a blurry trail behind objects? This phenomenon is called Ghosting.

Why does Ghosting happen?

Ghosting occurs when the pixels on your LCD/OLED panel cannot change colors fast enough to keep up with the moving image. This is governed by the Pixel Response Time (measured in milliseconds, ms).

  • 1ms (GtG): Ideal for gaming. Ghosting is virtually invisible.
  • 5ms - 10ms: Common in IPS/VA panels. Slight blur may be noticeable.
  • > 10ms: Significant trailing, bad for fast-paced games.

Optimization Guide: How to Boost Your FPS?

Is your FPS lower than expected? Follow these professional steps to optimize your system for maximum performance.

1. Enable Hardware Acceleration

Browsers like Chrome and Edge sometimes render graphics using the CPU to save battery. For this tool to be accurate, ensure "Use graphics acceleration when available" is turned ON in your browser settings.

2. Manage V-Sync (Vertical Sync)

V-Sync synchronizes your FPS with your monitor's refresh rate to prevent screen tearing.

  • Turn ON: For single-player story games where visuals matter more than speed.
  • Turn OFF: For competitive multiplayer games. V-Sync introduces "Input Lag", making your mouse movements feel delayed.

3. Thermal Throttling Check

If your FPS starts high (e.g., 60 FPS) but drops to 40 FPS after 10 minutes, your device is overheating. The CPU slows down to protect itself. Clean your PC's dust filters or use a cooling fan for your mobile device.
